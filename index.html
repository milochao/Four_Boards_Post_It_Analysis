Palo Alto Museum · community board analysis

At two community events, the museum put up four boards and asked four questions. Around 200 people answered on sticky notes.

This walks through what people wrote, sorts it into the desires underneath, and asks what each desire would mean if the museum built itself around it.

Board 1 · Common Ground Suggest speakers and performers you'd like to see here. Mostly famous names: tech founders, politicians, a few local musicians. People reached for prestige. But a handful asked for something smaller, like a coffee shop, or a place to actually gather.
Board 2 · Imagine a World Without Palo Alto What theme would you like in a future exhibition? The sharpest board. Alongside the tech milestones, people demanded the buried history: eugenics at Stanford, redlining, the Chinese railroad workers, the Ohlone, "anything at all on Black Palo Alto."
Board 3 · Museum in Your Pocket Add a story about Palo Alto for this mobile museum. Place-based memory. The orchards, the duck pond, lost schools, Juana Briones. People wanted the city's physical past held onto, not just its inventions.
Board 4 · From Hooves to Hollywood Name a creative figure connected with Palo Alto. The question asked for movie people. People named the whole creative economy: Pixar, IDEO, writers, the Grateful Dead. The sharpest answer is the oldest. Eadweard Muybridge shot the first motion photographs on a Palo Alto farm in 1878.

Act 4 · The five stances, tested against the research

Finding 1 · The public record, 2011 to 2023 The argument is already a decade old In a single comment thread, residents asked for exhibits on Stanford's eugenics history and redlining, and for Joan Baez and the Grateful Dead. The same people want both the hard history and the celebration. They are not separate camps.
Finding 2 · The museum building The buried history is already on site Juana Briones owned this land. A wall from her 1844 adobe house now stands at the museum's entrance. No other museum in the area can put a physical object like this at its front door.
Finding 3 · A study of 18 museums A half-hearted stance reaches no one The Computer History Museum has called itself a place "for everyone" for twenty years, and visitors still describe it as a museum for tech people. The Oakland Museum committed fully to its point of view and changed who walks in the door. Picking a clear stance is what works.
Finding 4 · The board and honorary chairs The bold ideas are on the letterhead, not in the room The honorary chairs are people known for reckoning with history and for public deliberation. The working board, the people who will actually run the museum, lean toward heritage and celebration. The cautious instinct is the one with the votes.
