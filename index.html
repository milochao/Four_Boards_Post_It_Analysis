Palo Alto Museum · community board analysis

At two community events, the museum put up six boards and asked six questions. Around 270 people answered on sticky notes.

This walks through what people wrote, sorts it by what they want the museum to do, and asks which of those the museum could build itself around.

Board 1 · Common Ground Suggest speakers and performers you'd like to see here. People reached for prestige: politicians, tech founders, local musicians. But a handful asked for something smaller — a coffee shop, a place to actually gather.
Board 2 · Museum in Your Pocket Add an interesting story about Palo Alto for this mobile museum. Place-based memory. Orchards, the duck pond, lost schools, Juana Briones. People wanted the city's physical past held — not just its inventions.
Board 3 · From Hooves to Hollywood Name a creative figure connected with Palo Alto. People named the whole creative economy: Pixar, IDEO, writers, the Grateful Dead. The sharpest answer is the oldest — Muybridge shot the first motion photographs on a Palo Alto farm in 1878.
Board 4 · The Life of Trees Add your ideas for interactive nature experiences for this exhibition. The board that asked about nature, and got nature back. Tree walks, Baylands, native plants. Almost all of it lives on this one board, which is the tell: it is mostly the question talking.
Board 5 · Imagine a World Without Palo Alto What theme would you like to see in a future artifact exhibition? The sharpest board. Alongside tech milestones: eugenics at Stanford, redlining, Chinese railroad workers, the Ohlone, "Anything at all on Black Palo Alto." And Theranos — the device that never worked — named as an artifact alongside the HP oscillator.
Board 6 · I Am Palo Alto Add the name of a Palo Altan whose story you'd like in this exhibition. The most specific board. Real names: Russell Lee MD, Birge Clark, Chanel Miller, Fran from Fran's Newsstand, the original nurses of the clinic. And at the bottom: "Let's Not ERASE History But Embrace it — so we can learn."

How to read this

Each board asked a question, and questions shape answers. The tree board produced trees, so size can be an echo of the prompt. The test is persistence: a theme that appears only where it was asked is mostly artifact; one that appears where it was not asked is real. Buried history passed. The tree cluster did not. These boards show what people want, not what they will attend, and they lean toward the already engaged. Interviews test both.

Finding 1 · The public record, 2011 to 2025 The argument is already a decade old In comment threads and on six community boards, residents have asked for the same things: the buried history alongside the celebration. They are not separate camps. They are the same people.
Finding 2 · The museum building The buried history is already on site Juana Briones owned this land. A wall from her 1844 adobe house stands at the museum's entrance. The original nurses of the clinic worked in this building. No other museum in the area opens with objects like these at its front door.
Finding 3 · A study of 18 museums A half-hearted stance reaches no one The Computer History Museum has called itself a place "for everyone" for twenty years, and visitors still describe it as a museum for tech people. The Oakland Museum committed fully to its point of view and changed who walks in the door.
Finding 4 · What the successful turnarounds did Commitment changed who came through the door In every documented case where a civic museum broke out of its inherited audience, it made a clear choice about what it would be and refused to hedge. The ones that tried to serve every camp at once stayed invisible to all of them.

buried history. The open question is how far to take it: surface what was hidden,

The live question for the room is whether the museum stops at surfacing or goes further and reckons. Built from six community boards, a decade of public comment, the Roth Building's own history, and a study of eighteen comparable institutions. A hypothesis the interviews test, not a verdict.
